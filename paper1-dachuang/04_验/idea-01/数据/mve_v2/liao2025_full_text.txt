A B S T R A C T
In this study, the effects of hot-air drying (HAD), hot-air combined microwave vacuum drying (HAD-MVD), 
vacuum freeze drying (VFD), and natural drying (ND) on the color, textural properties, microstructure, non- 
volatile substances, and volatile flavor compounds of the Morchella sextelata (M. sextelata) mushroom were 
investigated. The results showed that VFD best maintains the color, texture, and flavor of M. sextelata, while 
HAD-MVD yields dehydrated M. sextelata having a color and dense cellular microstructure similar to that ach­
ieved with VFD but in less time. In addition, the equivalent umami concentration (1293.20 g MSG/100 g) and 
total aroma compound content (2,533,403 μg/kg) for HAD-MVD were significantly higher than that for VFD, and 
the content of non-volatile substances such as soluble sugars, 5′-nucleotides, and total free amino acids were also 
high. Therefore, HAD-MVD represents an economic and more environmentally benign solution for the com­
mercial processing of M. sextelata.
1. Introduction
Morchella (Morchella sextelata (L). Pers) is a richly nutritious rare 
edible mushroom with a unique honeycomb pileus. However, it has a 
high water content and an active metabolism, making it susceptible to 
post-harvest environmental stress that can cause pileus deformation, 
softening, and browning. Accordingly, its natural shelf-life is less than 
one week (Li et al., 2023; Zhang, Li, Zhao, et al., 2023), necessitating the 
use of drying technology to extend its shelf-life while retaining its flavor.
Dehydration is commonly employed to maintain food quality and 
shelf-life (Liao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022), with many methods, such as 
natural drying (ND), hot-air drying (HAD) (Chen et al., 2023), micro­
wave vacuum drying (MVD) (Li et al., 2024), vacuum freeze drying 
(VFD) (Sun et al., 2023), and various novel combined drying technolo­
gies (Hu, Bi, Jin, Qiu, & Sman, 2021; Zhao et al., 2020) being applied to 
edible mushrooms. ND is the simplest and most traditional way of 
dehydrating edible mushrooms, and it remains the most widely used 
technique for small-scale cultivation, while HAD is the most commonly 
applied at the industrial scale. VFD is often used in the production of 
high-end dried Morchella sextelata (M. sextelata products).
With the ongoing improvement of health awareness around the 
world, consumer demand for high-quality, healthy, and nutritious food 
is growing. However, the dehydrated M. sextelata produced using ND 
and HAD is increasingly unable to meet current appearance and flavor 
standards. Therefore, producing high-quality dried M. sextelata at a 
lower cost is of paramount concern for M. sextelata processors.
Increasing research attention is being dedicated to the use of com­
bined drying processes for the production of dehydrated M. sextelata. 
Hot-air combined microwave vacuum drying (HAD-MVD) is a new type 
of combined drying method that involves a multi-stage drying process 
whereby HAD is used first to remove the free water on the surface of the 
material quickly and thus reduce drying time. Subsequently, MVD is

heat and mass-transfer effects. Specifically, microwaves penetrate deep 
into a material, so microwave heating does not rely on the heat con­
duction of the material itself, resulting in rapid internal heating and the 
formation of a large amount of water vapor. This high water-vapor- 
pressure gradient promotes the rapid movement of liquid and gaseous 
water from inside a sample to its surface. Accordingly, this combined 
drying method not only significantly increases drying rates, it also 
effectively reduces energy consumption and production costs (Pu & Sun, 
2017; Xu et al., 2020).
The drying process can also affect the unique flavor of edible 
mushrooms (Wen et al., 2022), which is due to the interaction of volatile 
organic compounds (VOCs) and non-volatile organic compounds (Aisala 
et al., 2020). VOCs include alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, esters, and 
sulfides, while non-volatile substances include soluble sugars, organic 
acids, free amino acids, and 5′-nucleotides (Hu, Feng, Huang, Ibrahim, & 
Liu, 2020). Many studies have shown that the flavor composition of 
edible mushrooms is significantly altered by their processing. For 
example, Hou et al. (2021) adopted headspace-gas chromatic-ion 
migration spectrometry to establish the volatile fingerprints of Lentinus 
edodes after HAD, VFD, and VFD-HAD treatments, revealing that VFD 
and VFD-HAD better retain volatile flavor substances, while HAD is 
subject to a large loss of volatile substances. Li et al. (2023) explored the 
effects of three drying methods (VFD, HAD, and ND) on the volatile 
components of M. sextelata. The results showed that alcoholic, hetero­
cyclic, and ketonic substances are greatly affected by drying method, 
with VFD being conducive to preserving flavor and HAD promoting the 
formation of baking flavor substances. Hu et al. (2020) investigated the 
effects of ND, HAD, and VFD on the non-volatile components of Stro­
pharia rugosoannulata, revealing that soluble sugars, organic acids, free 
amino acids, and 5′-nucleotide contents in VFD-treated mushroom were 
the highest, while HAD resulted in a higher equivalent umami concen­
tration (EUC) value.
Currently, changes in the flavor profiles of shiitake mushrooms (Hou 
et al., 2021), Flammulina velutipes (Sun et al., 2023), and Pleurotus eryngii 
(Li et al., 2015) after different drying treatments have been studied. 
Nevertheless, the effect of different drying techniques on the flavor of 
M. sextelata remains unclear. Therefore, the purpose of this study was to 
investigate the effects of HAD, VFD, HAD-MVD and ND on the volatile 
and non-volatile compounds of M. sextelata by using headspace–solid- 
phase microextraction (HS-SPME), gas chromatography–mass spec­
trometry (GC–MS), high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), 
and ion chromatography (IC). To our best knowledge, this study is the 
first to comprehensively analyze the volatile compounds and non- 
volatile compounds of M. sextelata dried by different drying methods, 
and to study the correlation between non-volatile compounds and vol­
atile compounds in M. sextelata during drying. The results of this study 
will provide theoretical basis for the production of dried M. sextelata 
products with unique flavor and delicious taste.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
Fresh M. sextelata mushrooms were harvested from Bole City, Xin­
jiang Uygur Autonomous Region in April 2023 and transported to the 
Food College of Shihezi University on the day of harvest, where they 
were stored at 3–5 ◦C until drying.
Glutaric dialdehyde, Ethanol absolute, Methanol (HPLC grade) and 
acetonitrile (HPLC grade) were purchased from Tianjin Xinbote Chem­
ical Co., Ltd. (Tianjin, China). 4-Methyl-2-pentanol (HPLC grade), 3- 
nitrophenylhydrazine (HPLC grade), Trichloromethane (HPLC grade) 
and formic acid (HPLC grade) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co. 
(St. Louis, U.S.A). All other chemicals were of analytical grade unless 
specified.
2.2. Sample treatment
The fresh M. sextelata were spread evenly and dried by one of the four 
different methods until the moisture content of the sample was below 12 
%.
2.3. Drying conditions and equipment
ND: 200 g fresh M. sextelata was evenly placed in a cool and venti­
lated place with a temperature of 25 ± 1 ◦C and a relative humidity of 52 
± 2 %. The drying time was ~60 h.
HAD: 200 g fresh M. sextelata were spread evenly on a tray and put in 
an electric blast drying oven (BGZ246, Shanghai Boxun Medical Bio­
logical Instrument Corp., Shanghai, China) at 55 ◦C for ~6 h.
VFD: 200 g fresh M. sextelata was evenly spread on a tray, cooled to 
−20 ◦C for 8 h, then placed in a vacuum freezing drying oven (ScientZ- 
10nd, Scientz Co., Ningbo, China) at −30 ◦C for 2 h. The vacuum 
pressure was 10 Pa and the cold trap temperature was −25 ◦C.
HAD-MVD: 200 g of fresh M. sextelata was evenly spread on a tray 
and placed in an electric blast drying oven at 55 ◦C until the moisture 
content of the sample dropped to 30 % (~195 min). Then, the material 
was evenly placed on a ceramic disk and immediately put into a mi­
crowave vacuum drying oven (RWBZ-08S, Nanjing Saenuri Drying 
Equipment Co., Ltd., Nanjing, China) at 55 ◦C with a power of 475 W for 
10 min. The pressure was −0.08-0.09 MPa.

Shenzhen, China) was used to determine the color changes of the pileus

2.4.2. Texture characteristics
The texture characteristics were measured using a texture analyzer

the sample deformation was 50 %, and the hardness (g), brittleness (f), 
elasticity (mm), and chewability (g) were measured. Each test was 
performed three times.

According to the method of and the internal microstructure of the 
dried samples at 50× and 300× was observed using a scanning electron

published method (Liao, Liu, et al., 2024).

2.4.5. Organic acids
Organic acids were determined according to a previously published 
method (Liao, Liu, et al., 2024). Briefly, 0.1–0.5 g of M. sextelata was 
extracted with 300 μL methanol/chloroform (7:3) on ice for 30 min. 
Then, 200 μL of H2O was added, the mixture was centrifuged for 10 min, 
and the supernatant was taken for analysis. This H2O extraction step was 
then repeated. Ultra-performance liquid chromatography (UPLC; 
Vanquish, Thermo, USA) and high-resolution mass spectrometry (Q 
Exactive, Thermo, USA) were employed with the aid of an autosampler 
at 4 ◦C. The chromatographic column used was a Waters BEH C18 (50 ×
2.1 mm, 1.8 μm). The mobile phase A was acetonitrile/ water (84:16, v/ 
v); mobile phase B was 10 mM ammonium formate; the flow rate was 
0.35 mL/min; the column temperature was 40 ◦C; and the injection 
volume was 2 μL. The results are expressed as mg/g DW.
2.4.6. Free amino acids
A 1 g sample was extracted with 50 mL hydrochloric acid (0.1 mol/L) 
for 45 min at 25 ◦C followed by centrifugation at 8000 ×g for 10 min. 
The supernatant was filtered, and analysis was performed using Bio­
chrom Ltd. (UK). The results are expressed as mg/g DW.
2.4.7. 5’-nucleotides
For the determination of 5′-nucleotides, a slightly modified previous 
method (Wen et al., 2022) was used. Briefly, 0.5 g of sample was sub­
jected to ultrasonic extraction in 25 mL water for 40 min then shaken in 
a 100 ◦C water bath for 1 h. Then, the mixture was cooled to room 
temperature (25 ◦C), centrifuged at 8400 ×g for 10 min, and the su­
pernatant was filtered. A Shimadzu LC-20 CE column equipped with an 
Ultimate AQ-C18 column (4.6 × 250 mm, 5 μm) was used for 5′-nucle­
otide analysis. The mobile phase was a 1:1 solution of KH2PO4 and 
K2HPO4 (pH 6.00) at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min, and the samples were 
detected at 254 nm. The results are expressed in mg/g DW.
2.4.8. Aroma-compound extraction and analysis
HS-SPME-GC–MS was used to detect volatile components according 
to a previously published method (Liao et al., 2024). First, 0.1 g of the 
sample was transferred to a 15 mL extraction bottle containing 5 mL 
ultrapure water and 10 μL of a 4-methyl-2-amyl alcohol internal stan­
dard solution. The sample bottle was placed on a solid-phase micro­
extraction device at 55 ◦C and preheated for 15 min. The SPME extracted 
fiber head was aged at 250 ◦C at the GC–MS inlet until there was no 
impurity peak, and extraction was performed for 30 min. Then, the 
samples were analyzed at 250 ◦C for 3 min.
An Agilent GC–MS system (7890 A-5975C, Agilent Technologies Inc., 
USA) fitted with a DB-WAX (30.0 mm × 250 μm, 0.25 μm) column was 
used for qualitative and quantitative analysis. The initial temperature 
was maintained at 50 ◦C for 1 min, increased to 120 ◦C at 5 ◦C/min 
where it was held for 2 min than increased to 240 ◦C at 10 ◦C/min and 
held for for 5 min. The gasification chamber temperature was 220 ◦C, 
the transmission line temperature was 240 ◦C, the carrier gas was He at a 
flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. No diversion was used. Mass spectroscopy was 
conducted in SCAN mode from 35 to 500 u with an electron ionization 
energy of 70 eV, an ion source temperature of 230 ◦C, and a quadrupole 
temperature of 150 ◦C.
2.4.9. Electronic-nose measurement
A PEN3 electronic nose (Airsense Analytics GmbH, Schwerin, Ger­
many) was used following a previously published method (Li, Zhou, 
et al., 2024). A 10 g sample was weighed into a 40 mL headspace bottle 
fitted with a Teflon cap. After 30 min at 37 ◦C, the headspace was 
injected into the device at a speed of 400 mL/min. The detection time 
was 80 s. The maximum output data for each sensor were used for 
further analysis.
2.4.10. Equivalent umami concentration (EUC)
EUC values, which are expressed as g MSG/100 g on a scale con­
structed using the umami intensity of different 5′-nucleotide and amino 
acid mixtures, are used to quantify the influence of different compounds 
on umami taste. In the current study, EUC values were calculated using 
the method described by (Yamaguchi, Yoshikawa, Ikeda, & Ninomiya, 
1971).
2.5. Statistical analysis
Origin 2022 (OriginLab, USA) software was used to organize and plot 
the data, and SPSS 22.0 (SPSS, Inc., Chicago, IL, USA) was used to 
analyze the difference significance of the data. One-way analysis of 
variance (ANOVA) was applied, followed by Duncan’s multi-range test. 
p < 0.05 was taken to represent significant difference. All experiments 
were performed three times, and the data are presented as mean ±
standard deviation. Peak areas of identified non-VOCs and VOCs were 
introduced into a free online platform named Omicshare (https://cloud. 
metware.cn) for multivariate analyses, including venn diagram, flavor 
distribution profile, circos plot, heat map and network correlation 
analysis.

For both HAD and HAD-MVD, heating may lead to non-enzymatic 
browning and proteins undergoing the Maillard reaction (Xu et al., 
2020), resulting in differences in color indicators. (Tian, Zhao, Huang, 
Zeng, & Zheng, 2016) reported less color degradation in HAD-MVD 
mushroom products relative to HAD shiitake mushrooms. Bai et al.

employing HAD-MVD can shorten drying time by increasing the drying 
rate, avoiding the browning caused by long-term heating. For the non- 
thermal drying method VFD, the overall color damage is small and the 
color difference for the pileus and stipes is only 1.61 and 5.74, indicating 
that a low drying temperature and the low-oxygen environment formed 
by vacuum retains the original color of M. sextelata to the maximum 
extent (Shen, He, Ding, & Sun, 2023).
3.1.2. Texture characteristics
The textural characteristics of dehydrated products directly affect 
the consumer’s eating experience (Liu et al., 2021). The effects of 
different drying methods on the hardness, chewiness, and springiness of 
M. sextelata are shown in Table 2. ND shows the lowest hardness and 
chewiness. The product is too soft and has lost most of its original texture 
characteristics. The chewiness of M. sextelata following FD treatment is 
highest, followed by that for HAD. It is lowest for HAD-MVD. The 
hardnesses of M. sextelata following the two thermal drying treatments 
HAD and HAD-MVD) are high at 2584.6 and 3567.5 g, respectively, 
while that for VFD-treated M. sextelata is only 1659.4 g. Wang, Jiang, 
et al. (2023) found that VFD-treated Lentinus edodes had lower hardness 
and chewiness when studying the influence of drying methods on the 
flavor substances of Flammulina velutipes.
These differences may be caused by the different moisture-transfer 
mechanisms of the three drying methods. For HAD and HAD-MVD, the 
diffusion of moisture proceeds from the inside to the surface where the 
sample solutes gradually concentrate. The subsequent intense heat 
treatment destroys the internal springiness of the M. sextelata, creating a 
hard surface (Xu et al., 2020). However, during the VFD process, ice 
crystals are formed inside the sample, leaving porous channels when 
sublimation occurs, resulting in a more resilient dry product (Zhou et al., 
2021).
3.1.3. Microstructure
The scanning electron microscopy images of the outer surface of the 
pileus are shown in Fig. 1, where Fig. 1A1–E1 show a fresh sample and 
those subjected to different drying methods. Figure 1A2 (50×) and A3 
(300×) show the microstructure of the M. sextelata FS. The villi on the 
surface of the fresh M. sextelata are three-dimensional and evenly 
distributed, the cell-wall structure is complete, and the overall 
morphology is smooth and dense. Under 50× magnification, obvious 
cracks and indentations are observed in the fruiting body of M. sextelata 
treated with HAD (Fig. 1B1 and B2). Villi collapse and atrophy of the 
fruiting body are observed at 300×, and the cell-wall and fiber structure 
are seriously damaged. Due to the long-term high-temperature state, the 
surface formed a hard shell due to the rapid loss of water, and the 
accumulation of water vapor and energy in the internal tissues was 
excessive, resulting in the damage of the cell wall support function, the 
contraction of water exchange channels, and a wide range of internal 
collapse (Chen, Achkar, Liu, & Bennacer, 2021).
As shown in Fig. 1C1 and C2, the surface of the fruiting body of the 
sample obtained by HAD-MVD treatment is relatively complete. 
Compared with the HAD-treatment group, surface cracking is signifi­
cantly reduced, the cell structure is dense, and most of the villi have 
maintained morphology. The spatial morphology of the VFD samples 
(Fig. 1D1 and D2) is stereoscopic, with good cell integrity and less 
compressed cell structure. The cell structure for VFD samples is closest to 
that for FS. This is consistent with the findings reported in Sun et al. 
(2023) and Wang, Jiang, et al. (2023), who showed that VFD treatment 
results in better microscopic spatial structure and integrity. According to 
Fig. 1E1 and E2, ND causes serious damage, cracks, and depressions, and 
the spatial structure is collapsed. This is due to the ND treatment time 
being too long, which leads to tissue shrinkage. At the same time, the

3.2. Changes in taste substances
3.2.1. Soluble sugars
Soluble sugars are important species for the quality and flavor of 
edible mushrooms (Hu et al., 2020). In the present study, the total sol­
uble sugar contents of the M. sextelata samples are increased for all 
groups except ND, with the highest being 350.00 mg/g DW in the VFD- 
treated group, followed by HAD-MVD (338.39 mg/g DW), HAD (229.14 
mg/g DW), and ND (202.59 mg/g DW) (p < 0.05). The most abundant 
soluble sugars in edible mushrooms are mannose, glucose, arabinose, 
and trehalose (Zhang et al., 2023). Arabinose was reported to be the 
major soluble sugar in Stropharia rugosoannulata (Hu et al., 2020) and 
mannose is the major soluble sugar in dried shiitake mushrooms (Liu 
et al., 2022). However, the highest-content soluble sugar in M. sextelata 
is trehalose (Table 3), which is a non-reducing disaccharide in which a 
α,α-1,1-glycosidic connects two glucose units. Trehalose has attracted 
much attention from researchers because of its ability to maintain cell 
homeostasis and alleviate cell damage (Elbein, 2003). In this study, 
trehalose accounted for 88 %–98 % of the total soluble sugars in 
different treatment groups. Interestingly, in ND-treated M. sextelata, the 
trehalose content is decreased significantly while the glucose content is 
increased significantly (p < 0.05), which may be due to the activity of 
trehalase – the enzyme that promotes trehalose decomposition into 
glucose – being promoted under ND conditions (Elbein, 2003).
3.2.2. Organic acids
Organic acids produce sour and astringent flavors and play a crucial 
role in mushroom flavor formation (Liu, Bau, et al., 2022). The organic 
acid contents for fresh and dried M. sextelata are shown in Table 3. 
Compared with FS, the organic acid contents of dried M. sextelata are 
significantly increased (p < 0.05) with the degree of increase following 
the trend HAD > HAD-MVD > VFD > ND. The VFD-treated samples have 
a low total organic acid content, indicating that they are less acidic and 
astringent. However, the total organic acid contents for both the HAD 
and HAD-MVD groups are significantly increased, enhancing the sour 
and astringent taste of M. sextelata to a certain extent as well as its 
overall flavor (organic acid and soluble sugar play a synergistic role to 
enhance mushroom flavor) (Hu et al., 2020).
Malic acid, citric acid, and fumaric acid are the main organic acids in 
M. sextelata, accounting for 72 %–81 % of the total organic acid content, 
among which malic acid (9.46–17.49 mg/g DW) is highest, accounting 
for more than 34 % of the total content. This is followed by citric acid 
(7.11–9.05 mg/g DW) and fumaric acid (3.44–6.99 mg/g DW). The 
contents of these three organic acids in the M. sextelata treated with HAD 
and HAD-MVD are significantly higher than that in the VFD- and ND- 
treatment groups, which might be because the increase in temperature 
for these methods increases the activities of pyruvate kinase and pyru­
vate dehydrogenase, which are related to respiratory metabolism in the 
samples (Wen et al., 2022), and the accumulation of tricarboxylic acid 
cycle metabolizing intermediates (such as citric acid, fumaric acid, and 
succinic acid) during drying. Similar results were reported by Liu, Bau, 
et al. (2022), who found that heat treatment induces an increase in malic 
acid, citric acid, and succinic acid contents in shiitake mushrooms. 
Compared with HAD, the acidity and astringency of M. sextelata treated 
with HAD-MVD are lower, which is mainly attributed to the fact that 
HAD-MVD treatment effectively shortening the duration of hot air 
drying, thus reducing the accumulation of organic acids.

3.2.3. Free amino acids
Free amino acids are key substances in the flavor of edible mush­
rooms. Table 3 show the effects of different drying methods on the 
contents of 17 free amino acids in M. sextelata. The content of total free 
amino acids for FS is 55.70 mg/g DW, and the contents of total free 
amino acids in samples treated by different drying methods decrease in 
the order of VFD > HAD-MVD > HAD > ND. In general, drying can 
promote the release of free amino acids from proteins (Hu et al., 2020). 
Among the samples, the VFD-treated group has the highest content of 
free amino acids (57.62 mg/g DW), which may be related to the low 
temperature and vacuum environment slowing the Maillard reaction. Li, 
Zhou, et al. (2024) demonstrated that proteolytic enzymes can remain 
active at lower temperatures and can decompose proteins into free 
amino acids sustainably during freeze-drying. However, the contents of 
free amino acids in the HAD-MVD and HAD samples are low, which may 
be due to the influence of heat promoting the Maillard reaction.
Free amino acids can be divided into four categories according to 
taste characteristics: umami (aspartate and glutamate), sweet (threo­
nine, serine, glycine, alanine, and proline), bitter (valine, methionine, 
isoleucine, leucine, phenylalanine, histidine, and arginine), and tasteless 
(lysine, cystine, and tyrosine) (Wen et al., 2022). In general, umami 
amino acids are the most important for the flavor quality of dried 
M. sextelata products. In this study, glutamate – the main amino acid of 
umami flavor – is the highest amino acid detected in M. sextelata sam­
ples, accounting for 65 %–75 % of umami amino acids. The glutamate 
contents of the VFD and HAD-MVD samples are highest (9.91 and 9.19 
mg/g DW, respectively), followed by those for HAD (8.58 mg/g DW) and 
ND (7.74 mg/g DW). Thus, VFD and HAD-MVD treatments retain the 
umami substances of M. sextelata to the maximum extents (Li et al., 
2015).
3.2.4. 5’-nucleotides
5’-Nucleotides are an important component of the characteristic 
taste of edible mushrooms, and they are also precursors to some volatile 
components (Wen et al., 2022). As shown in Table 3, five 5′-nucleotides 
were detected in the samples, namely 5’-CMP, 5’-UMP, 5’-GMP, 5’-IMP, 
and 5’-AMP. Among them, 5’-CMP was detected only in dry samples, 
and 5’-GMP and 5’-IMP accounted for large proportions of the total 5′- 
nucleotide contents. The total 5′-nucleotide content for the FS group is 
5.11 mg/g, while those for the other samples are slightly increased 
(except that for ND). The VFD sample has the highest 5′-nucleotide 
content at 7.13 mg/g, which is attributed to its low temperature drying 
process and porous product structure preventing nucleotide degradation 
(Hu et al., 2020). Similar results were also observed in Stropharia rugoso- 
annulata (Hu et al., 2020) and Pleurotus eryngii (Li et al., 2015). The 5′- 
nucleotide content of the HAD sample is significantly lower than that for 
VFD and HAD-MVD (p < 0.05), which may be due to the destruction of 
5′-nucleotides exposed to long drying times and high temperature. 
Umami 5′-nucleotides include 5’-AMP, 5’-GMP, and 5’-IMP. Previous 
studies have shown that 5’-GMP and 5’-IMP are major contributors to 
umami taste, and there is a synergistic effect between them, while 5’- 
AMP can inhibit bitter taste, enhance sweetness, and contribute to 
umami taste (Luo et al., 2021). Among all the treatment groups, HAD- 
MVD samples show the highest 5’-GMP and 5’-AMP contents (3.98 
and 0.48 mg/g, respectively), while the highest 5’-IMP content (4.08 
mg/g) is observed for VFD. In the samples treated with VFD and HAD- 
MVD, the total contents of the above three nucleotides is significantly 
higher than that of HAD, which further indicates that VFD and HAD- 
MVD treatments confer desirable sweet and fresh flavor characteristics 
to M. sextelata.
The above results show that there are significant differences in the 
non-volatile compounds in the M. sextelata products dried with different 
treatments, with those treated with VFD and HAD-MVD having higher 
levels of soluble sugars, free amino acids, and 5′-nucleotides, which 
significantly enhance the sweetness, freshness, and overall flavor of the 
products. However, in the samples treated with HAD, the content of 
organic acids is highest, and the interaction of organic acids with other 
flavor components (e.g., sugars, free amino acids, nucleotides, etc.) can 
form a more complex and varied flavor hierarchy. The reasons for the 
variation of these non-volatile compounds are complex because they 
interact with each other and may be involved in enzymatic, thermal, and 
oxidative reactions as precursors, resulting in different flavor profiles 
(Zhao et al., 2023).
EUC values indicate a synergistic effect between umami nucleotides 
and amino acids on umami properties (Hou et al., 2021). EUC is divided 
into four levels: > 1000 (grade 1), 100–1000 (grade 2), 10–100 (grade 
3), and < 10 g MSG/100 g (grade 4) (Li et al., 2015). Table 3 shows the 
EUC values of the M. sextelata samples. The EUC values of fresh, HAD, 
and ND samples are grade 2. HAD-MVD and VFD methods increase the 
EUC value to grade 1, and there is no significant difference between 
them (p > 0.05). This is because both HAD-MVD and VFD increase the 
glutamate and 5’-GMP contents in M. sextelata (an important source of 
umami) (Li et al., 2015). In addition, the 5’-AMP content for the HAD- 
MVD samples is higher than that of the VFD samples, suggesting that 
HAD-MVD is a potential technique for enhancing umami intensity, 
which is essential for the development of dried M. sextelata.

more uniform internal structure, which ensures that enzymes, such as 
lipoxygenase and hydroperoxide lyase, fully react with the unsaturated

Aldehydes, especially C5–C9 aldehydes, produced by the oxidation 
of unsaturated fatty acids, are also important components for the char­
acteristic aroma of edible mushrooms (Li et al., 2023). The highest 
content of aldehydes was found in the HAD-MVD-treated group 
(171,328 μg/kg), followed by the VFD-treated group (124,423 μg/kg). 
The highest-content aldehydes are benzaldehyde and E-2-octenal, which 
contribute to the bitter almond flavor and sweetness of the M. sextelata. 
The contents of benzaldehyde and E-2-octenal are significantly higher 
(p < 0.05) after HAD-MVD treatment, which may be related to the 
Strecker degradation of amino acids under high-temperature conditions 
and during microwave radiation (Hofmann, Münch, & Schieberle, 
2000). The higher content of aldehydes in the VFD-treated group is 
mainly attributed to the fact that the water in the M. sextelata in the 
drying process is directly sublimated in a low-temperature and low- 
pressure environment, so the internal organization of the tissue is 
more complete, which is conducive to the production and retention of 
volatile compounds. This is consistent with the findings reported in 
(Polat, Guclu, Kelebek, Keskin, & Selli, 2022).
A total of four ketones were detected in dried M. sextelata products, 
among which 3-octanone and 1-octen-3-one, as the main eight‑carbon 
ketones in M. sextelata, exude mushroomy and sweet flavors and a 
pleasant aroma. Zhang et al. (2023) also obtained that these substances 
when using ultra performance tandem mass spectrometry combined 
with two-dimensional gas chromatography-time-of-flight mass spec­
trometry method for the detection and relative quantification of me­
tabolites from two morchella varieties. Ketones are mainly produced by 
amino acid degradation, unsaturated fatty acid oxidation, and the 
Maillard reaction (Sun, Lin, Li, & Li, 2024). 3-Octanone (36,812 μg/kg) 
and 1-octen-3-one (59,092 μg/kg) are more abundant in the HAD-MVD 
sample than in the other treatment groups. This may be due to the fact 
that during the vacuum microwave treatment, the tissue cells are rapidly 
expanded under vacuum and high internal vapor pressure, resulting in 
the destruction of the cell walls, facilitating the exit of volatile 
compounds from cells (Liu, Liao, et al., 2022).
In order to further clarify the effect of different drying methods on 
the VOCs of M. sextelata, the variables were normalized to obtain a 
clustered heat map of different M. sextelata samples (Fig. 2D). The more 
intense the orange color, the higher the peak content, while the more 
intense the blue color, the lower the peak content. These differences in 
color and intensity visually reflect differences between multiple sample 
groups, facilitating the identification of similarities and differences be­
tween samples (Sun et al., 2024). As the Euclidean distance increases, 
distinctions between the characteristic volatile compounds of each 
group of samples clearly emerge.
Different drying treatments cause changes in the types and contents 
of volatile compounds in M. sextelata, and HAD-MVD and VFD increase 
the types and contents of alcohols and aldehydes in dried M. sextelata 
products. However, HAD-MVD presents the highest content of volatile 
aroma compounds, so HAD-MVD is a very promising way of drying 
M. sextelata from the point of view of volatile compounds.
3.3.2. Electronic nose analysis

reviews the total odor information of the examined samples (Li et al.,

sulfur compounds; aromatic and organic sulfur compounds; and nitro­

other samples, followed by the HAD-MVD-treated group, and this is in

4. Conclusions
The use of different drying methods results in significant differences 
in the sensory quality of M. sextelata, with the color and microstructure 
of VFD-treated M. sextelata being closer to those of fresh M. sextelata, and 
the contents of soluble sugars, 5′-nucleotides, and total free amino acids 
being at higher levels in the treatment groups. In addition, VFD treat­
ment increases the variety of volatile flavor substances, thus enriching 
the unique flavor of M. sextelata, but VFD treatment involves a long 
drying cycle, high energy consumption, and high production cost. 
Therefore, it is not widely used in commercial production. However, 
HAD-MVD treatment involves a shorter drying cycle and produces 
M. sextelata with a color and structure similar to that achieved with VFD. 
Furthermore, HAD-MVD treatment significantly increases the contents 
of total soluble sugars, 5′-nucleotides, total free amino acids, and volatile 
flavor substances in M. sextelata. In addition, the electronic nose results 
were consistent with the changes in flavor compounds, suggesting that 
HAD-MVD and VFD treatments improve the unique flavor of 
M. sextelata. The results of HAD-MVD were better than those of VFD in 
terms of EUC values and total aroma compound content. Correlation 
analysis showed that there is a significant association between the for­
mation of non-volatile and volatile flavor substances in M. sextelata (p <
0.05). Therefore, considering actual production in terms of time and 
cost, HAD-MVD is an ideal process for drying M. sextelata.
